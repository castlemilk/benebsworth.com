## The core idea

A **phase-locked loop** is a feedback circuit that forces a local oscillator to match the frequency *and* phase of an incoming signal. It is one of the most quietly ubiquitous structures in engineering — every radio front-end, CPU clock multiplier, WiFi chip, and grid-tied inverter contains one. The trick is elegant: compare phases, filter the difference, and use that error to steer the oscillator. When the loop locks, the local oscillator becomes a clean, noise-rejecting copy of a reference it may only see buried in noise.

</LabSide>

## How it works

A PLL chains three blocks into a loop:

1. **Phase detector (PD)** — produces an error from the phase difference. The classic sinusoidal detector outputs `e = sin(θref − θvco)`, which pushes the loop to *align* the two phases rather than drive them to zero.
2. **Loop filter** — smooths the error. A bare gain gives a type-I loop; adding an integrator gives type-II.
3. **VCO** — a voltage-controlled oscillator whose frequency is `ω = ω₀ + K·v`.

Here the reference is a **chirp**, sweeping slowly across the VCO's free-running frequency so you can watch acquisition happen. Lock is reached when $\theta_{ref} - \theta_{vco}$ stops growing and the phase-error trace flattens onto zero.

<LabSide effect="pll-lock-in" height={280} params={{ Kp: 1, order: "2" }} reverse={true} caption="Lower bandwidth (K=1) — slower lock, but the loop ignores more noise.">

Drop the loop bandwidth K and the PLL takes longer to capture the chirp, but it filters more aggressively. That is the central PLL trade-off: a wide loop locks fast and tracks jitter; a narrow loop locks slowly and rejects it.

</LabSide>

## Type-I vs type-II

The filter order changes the loop's *character*, not just its speed:

- **Type-I** (proportional only) locks quickly but leaves a **static phase error** — it needs a steady offset to hold the VCO at the right frequency.
- **Type-II** (PI filter) adds an integrator that accumulates the error until the steady-state phase error is driven to **zero**. This is the default for clock recovery, frequency synthesis, and demodulation, where residual phase offset is unacceptable.

## Where it shows up

- **Frequency synthesis** — multiply a cheap crystal up to GHz local-oscillator tones.
- **Clock recovery** — USB, PCIe, and Ethernet extract a clock straight from the data edges.
- **FM demodulation** — a PLL locked to an FM carrier outputs a voltage proportional to instantaneous frequency.
- **Grid synchronisation** — inverters lock to the 50/60 Hz mains before pushing current into it.

</LabSide>

## The knobs

- **Loop bandwidth K** — the overall loop gain. Higher K means faster acquisition and tighter tracking, at the cost of more noise let through; lower K is slower but quieter.
- **Filter order** — choose **1st order** (proportional, type-I, leaves a static phase error) or **2nd order** (PI, type-II, drives the steady-state phase error to zero).

Changing either control re-arms the chirp so you can replay the whole acquisition transient.
